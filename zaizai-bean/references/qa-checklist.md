# QA Checklist

## Required Item

- It's a 16:9 landscape format.
- The background is a clean white background.
- There's a little brown one.
- ZaiZai Bean is responsible for the core actions, not just for decoration.
- Instead of replicating the composition of the old case, it generated new metaphors for the current case study.
- The visuals are bizarre, creative, and interesting.
- Simple and clean, with the main subject occupying no more than 60% of the image.
- One diagram illustrates only one core structure.
- English annotations are few, short, and easy to read.
- Orange is only used for main paths or arrows.
- Red is only used for important points, questions, reminders, or results.
- Blue is only used for supplementary explanations, feedback, or system status.

## Failure signal

Regenerate or partially edit if the following occurs:
- The top left corner has headings such as "Common Pitfalls / Workflow / System Architecture Diagram / Roadmap".
- The little brown creature resembles a mascot, an emoji, or a cute cartoon character.
- The presentation resembles a PowerPoint presentation, courseware, or a formal flowchart.
- Too many elements, too many arrows, too many nodes.
- The text was transformed into lengthy explanations.
- The background features paper texture, shadows, gradients, beige, and noise.
- Realistic UI screenshots or tech-inspired interfaces.
- The English characters are seriously misspelled or the annotations are unreadable.
- The visuals are too rigid and lack any absurd metaphors.
- The compositions in `assets/examples/` the old cases are too similar to those in the original.

## Iteration Methods

- Too ordinary: Make ZaiZai Bean the main subject of the action and add a strange but valid metaphor.
- Too complicated: Delete nodes and keep only one action and 3-5 short annotations.
- Too cute: Emphasizes deadpan, blank serious expression, not cute, not mascot.
- Too much PPT: Remove the title, borders, neat grid, and excessive arrows, and replace it with a hand-drawn scene.
- Too similar to old cases: retain the core meaning, but change the main object and the little brown character's actions.
- Text errors: prioritize local editing; if there are many errors, regenerate and reduce the number of annotations.

## Delivery judgement

A high-quality diagram should initially seem "a bit strange" to the reader, but allow them to understand its structure within one second.

If it looks like a tutorial page at first glance, rather than a bizarre product sketch on a blank sheet of paper, it's not acceptable.
